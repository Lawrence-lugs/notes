Proper Time

Proper time is the time a moving particle experiences, because it’s defined as \[d\tau^2=-ds^2=-d\vec{x}\cdot d\vec{x}\] which has a consequence such that \[\frac{d\vec{x}}{d\tau}=(\vec{e_0})_{MCRF}\] Which is the time-basis of the “tangent reference frame” to the particle.

What is acceleration?

From the twins paradox, we know that both the stationary twin and the moving twin will observe that each other has a slower clock. However, when the moving twin “turns back” to travel towards the stationary twin, the stationary twin’s clock will have seemed like it ticked a lot instantly.

This means that the act of accelerating makes every other clock seem instantly faster.

Is this explainable by pure special relativity without curvature?

Tensors

The one form: \[f_\alpha:=\mathbf{f}(\vec{e_\alpha})\] The two-form: \[f_{\alpha\beta}:=\mathbf{f}(\vec{e_\alpha},\vec{e_\beta})\] \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{f}(\vec{A},\vec{B})=\mathbf{f}(A^\alpha\vec{e_\alpha},B^\beta\vec{e_\beta})\\ =A^\alpha B^\beta \mathbf{f} ( \vec{e_\alpha},\vec{e_\beta} ) \\ =A^\alpha B^\beta f_{\alpha\beta} \end{aligned}\] That is, all forms are just functions of increasing numbers of basis sets because they work on N vectors (for the N-form).

Just like a matrix, the two-form tensor is a sum over simple outer-products.

The Energy-Momentum 4-vector

This is called so because the definition of 4-momentum \[\vec{p}=m\vec{U}\] and since the 4-velocity \(\vec{U}\) is just \[\vec{U}=\{1,v_x,v_y,v_z\}\] this immediately leads to the discovery that its components \[p_\alpha=\{E,p_x,p_y,p_z\}\] are energy and the 3 spatial momenta.

In non-relativistic units, the first time would be \(E/c\).

This is because the time-component of \(\vec U\) is \(c\) in special relativity- which is further because \(ct\) is the unit of the time axis that we use.

Stress-Energy Tensor

\[T_{\alpha\beta}\] Its \(00\) is energy, \(0i\) and \(i0\) are symmetric and describe momentum and energy flux, which is just momentum. \(ij\) are momentum flux.

In fluids without heat transfer or viscosity (ideal space dust), the stress-energy tensor receives a \(0\) in momentum flux unless \(i=j\) where we are describing energy transfer via momentum through a surface.

Then, in such ideal fluids, stress-energy becomes:

\[T_{\alpha \beta}=(\rho+p)U^\mu U^\nu + pg^{\mu\nu}\]

The form above is for a ideal fluid. Which, technically at large scale, anything in the universe is. (Except, we have to account for viscosity sometime.)

Christoffel Symbols

When we tried to differentiate a vector, we

We define the regular derivative (the derivative of each component of a vector): \[V^\alpha_{,\beta}=\frac{dV^\alpha}{dx^\beta}\vec{e^\alpha}\] This is the vector derivative in Cartesian Coordinates.

We can differentiate an arbitrary vector in arbitrary coordinates, but we found that some sort of product rule applies: \[\frac{d\vec{V}}{dx^\beta}=\frac{dV^\alpha}{dx^\beta}\vec{e^\alpha}+V^\alpha \frac{d\vec{e^\alpha}}{dx^\beta}\] We call this the covariant derivative.

In this equation, we use christoffel symbols instead to describe the change in basis against a change in the coordinates. \[V{^\alpha_{;\beta}} =V^\alpha_{,\beta}+\Gamma^\alpha_{\mu \beta} V^\mu\] Where, if the coordinates are not Cartesian, the Christoffel symbols \(\Gamma\) pop out to describe the shifting of the basis with respect to the origin.

They are a tensor describing how the basis changes with respect to coordinate change. \[\Gamma^\mu_{\alpha \beta}\vec{e_\mu}=\frac{d}{dx^\beta}\vec{e^\alpha}\]

Usefully, the Christoffel symbols can be calculated from the metric via:

\[\Gamma^\mu_{\alpha \beta}=\frac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}(g_{\nu \beta,\alpha}+g_{\alpha \nu,\beta}-g_{\alpha \beta,\nu})\]

Metric Volume/Length Element

It would make sense that the volume element in a manifold after coordinate transform would be: \[dx^{4'}=\det(g_{\alpha \beta})dx^4\] We typically refer to this determinant as \(g\)

The length of a curve is measured via:

\[\int_{\lambda_0}^{\lambda_1}|\vec{V}\cdot\vec{V}|d\lambda\] if \(\lambda\) is the parameter of the curve and \(\vec{V}\) is the vector tangent to the curve.

When we say \(\lambda\) is the parameter of the curve, the curve is defined as:

\[:\{\xi=f(\lambda),\eta=g(\lambda),\lambda_0\leq\lambda\leq\lambda_1\}\]

In differential geometry, we find a unique not-necessarily-unit tangent vector to any curve via: \[\frac{d\phi}{d\lambda}=<\tilde{d\phi},\vec{V}>\] and hence

\[\vec{V}=\left\{ \frac{d\xi}{d\lambda},\frac{d\eta}{d\lambda} \right\} \]

Geodesics

In a differentiable manifold that may be curved, the definition of a “line that always stays in the same direction” is in doubt.

As a proxy, the concept of enforcing parallel transport of the tangent vector is introduced. That is,

\[\nabla_{\vec{U}}\vec{V}=0\] where this notation means we take the derivative along the world line \[U^\beta V^\alpha_{;\beta}\] Iff \(\vec{U}\) is tangent to a geodesic \[\nabla_{\vec U}\vec{U}=0\] Then if \(U^\alpha=dx^\alpha/d\lambda\) and \(U^\beta \partial/\partial x^\beta=d/d\lambda\) then this condition leads to something that looks like the euler-lagrange equation only this time with a Christoffel symbol.

\[\frac{d}{d\lambda}\left( \frac{dx^\alpha}{d\lambda} \right) + \Gamma^\alpha_{\mu \beta}\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}\frac{dx^\beta}{d\lambda}=0\]

Riemann Tensor

We can “measure” the curvature of a manifold by measuring how much the direction of the parallel transport changes over a closed loop.

This would give us a change in \(V^\alpha\) due to transport around the loop. \[\delta V^\alpha=\delta a \delta b \left[ { \Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\sigma,\lambda} - \Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\lambda,\sigma} - \Gamma^\alpha_{\nu\lambda} \Gamma^\nu_{\mu\sigma} - \Gamma^\alpha_{\nu\sigma} \Gamma^\nu_{\mu\lambda} } \right] V^\mu \] Since \(V^\mu\) is a vector, we can think that the entire left side is the basis one-form that gives \(\delta V^\alpha\) from the vector \(\delta \vec{V}\).

\[R^\alpha_{\beta\mu\nu}=\text{set of christoffel terms}\] \[R^\alpha_{\mu \lambda \sigma} = { \Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\sigma,\lambda} - \Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\lambda,\sigma} - \Gamma^\alpha_{\nu\lambda} \Gamma^\nu_{\mu\sigma} - \Gamma^\alpha_{\nu\sigma} \Gamma^\nu_{\mu\lambda} }\] We call this the Riemann tensor.

With the form of the Riemann tensor, we can see the following:

  • It has 3 lower (one-form) indices and 1 upper index.

3 lower indices means that if we give it 3 vectors, it becomes a vector.

The 3 vectors are \(V^\mu\), \(\delta a \vec{e_\mu}\), \(\delta b \vec{e_\nu}\).

In the end, we see that the Riemann curvature takes the infinitesimal area and a vector (any) as argument and gives a vector pointing out the curvature.

In a locally inertial frame \(P\): \[R^\alpha_{\beta \mu \nu} = \frac{1}{2}g^{\sigma \sigma}(g_{\sigma \nu, \beta \mu}-g_{\sigma \nu, \beta \nu} + g_{\beta \mu, \sigma \nu} - g_{\beta \nu, \sigma \mu})\]

Ricci Tensor

This identity is verifiable

\[R_{\alpha \beta \mu \nu}=-R_{\beta \alpha \mu \nu}\] and so if two of the indices are the same, then \(A=-A=0\) \[R_{\alpha\alpha\mu\nu}=0\] It’s also verifiable that all other index repetitions would result in the Riemann tensor disappearing to 0. EXCEPT THE RICCI TENSOR.

The Ricci tensor is ONLY nonzero contraction of the curvature tensor on the first upstairs and second downstairs index:

\[R_{\alpha \beta} = R^\mu_{\sigma \mu \beta}\] Applying the Ricci contraction,

\[R_{\mu\nu}=g^{\alpha\beta}R_{\alpha \mu \beta \nu}\]

and then further contracting gives the Ricci scalar:

\[R=g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}\]

The Ricci scalar is the double trace of the Ricci tensor.

Einstein Tensor and Field Equation

Contracting the Bianchi Identities twice with the Ricci contraction turns the bianchi identities into:

\[R_{;\lambda}-R^\mu_{\lambda;\mu}-R^\mu_{\lambda;\mu}=0\] (Turning the Ricci scalar contravariant derivative into an identity matrix reduction…) \[(2R^\mu_\lambda-\delta^\mu_\lambda R)_{;\mu}=0\] (divide this by 2 and we get…) and the Einstein Tensor can be defined to be

\[G^{\alpha\beta} \equiv R^{\alpha \beta}-\frac{1}{2}g^{\alpha \beta}R=G^{\beta\alpha}\] because the metric tensor in (1 1) form is the identity, so if we raise everything we can get the metric tensor back.

The last one gives us \[G^{\alpha \beta}_{;\beta}=0\] Einsten would later show that this final tensor which is derived from the more pure Riemann curvature is the one that mathematically causes gravity: \[G^{\alpha \beta}=8\pi T^{\alpha \beta}\] because it is related to the stress-energy tensor- which itself encodes the total energy-mass content in space.

Computing the Apparent Horizon

The apparent horizon is the outermost marginally outer trapped surface. Here, \(\Theta\) (expansion of a congruence of outgoing null geodesics) \(=0\).

That is, at this timeslice, the photons never point outward.

We can express the apparent horizon as a parametric surface \(r(\theta,\phi)\).

By spectral composition using spherical harmonics, \[r(\theta,\phi)=\sum_{l=0}^L \sum_{m=-l}^{l} a_{lm}Y_{lm}(\theta,\phi)\] > In spherical harmonics, \(m\) can only range from \(-l\) to \(l\)

Then, the problem becomes finding \(a_{lm}\) coefficients.

So then, we need to find that \(a_{lm}\) provides a surface where \(\Theta=0\).

\[\Theta=s^i_{;i}+K_{ij}s^is^j-K=0\] We use iterative solvers to enforce that this is 0.

\[a_{lm}^{(new)}=a_{lm}^{(old)}-\delta a_{lm}\]
